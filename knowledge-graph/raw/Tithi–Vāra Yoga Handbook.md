 How to use this handbook
 Use this as a quick reference when a Panchāṅga gives you a weekday and tithi. First identify the Vāra, then identify the Tithi, then check whether that pair creates Amrit Yog, Siddha Yog, or one of the inauspicious Vāra–Tithi yogas.
 
 Find the Vāra.Example: Thursday is Guru-vāra, ruled by Jupiter.
 Find the Tithi name.Example: Chaturthī belongs to the Riktā group.
 Check the master table.Thursday + Chaturthī appears under Amrit Yog because Thursday + Riktā Tithi forms Amrit Yog.
 Check for conflict.Some combinations can be auspicious in one rule and inauspicious in another. Do not treat one label as the whole muhurta.
 
 Scope note: This handbook covers only Vāra + Tithi yogas. A complete muhurta also examines Nakshatra, Yoga, Karana, Tara Bala, Chandra Bala, Lagna, planetary strength, avoidance periods, and the nature of the activity.
 

 
 The five Tithi groups
 Classical muhurta practice groups the tithis into five repeating families: Nandā, Bhadrā, Jayā, Riktā, and Pūrṇā. These groups are the backbone of the Amrit and Siddha Vāra–Tithi rules.

 Inauspicious Vāra–Tithi yogas
 The same weekday–tithi pairing can also create adverse yogas. These should trigger caution before choosing a muhurta, especially for auspicious beginnings such as marriage, house entry, business launch, signing, purchase, travel, or spiritual initiations.
 
 Krakacha YogaLiteral sense: saw, cutting, grating.Practical reading: considered among the harshest Vāra–Tithi yogas. Avoid for major constructive beginnings.
 Dagdha YogaLiteral sense: burnt.Practical reading: the tithi is treated as scorched or weakened for auspicious work.
 Hutasana YogaLiteral sense: fire, oblation-eating fire.Practical reading: fiery, consuming, unsuitable for peaceful or growth-oriented starts.
 Visha YogaLiteral sense: poison.Practical reading: contaminating or toxic influence; avoid for benefic activities.
 Samvartaka YogaLiteral sense: dissolution, destruction.Practical reading: treated as one of the worst Vāra–Tithi yogas; appears only in two listed combinations here.
 
 

 
 When auspicious and inauspicious rules overlap
 Important: An Amrit or Siddha label is not an automatic green signal. Some tithis can simultaneously fall under an auspicious group-based yoga and a named inauspicious yoga.

 Recommended rule of thumb
 Use Amrit and Siddha Yogas as supportive signals, not final approval. Use Dagdha, Visha, Hutasana, Krakacha, and Samvartaka as caution flags. The final muhurta should be decided only after checking purpose-specific suitability and the remaining panchāṅga limbs.
 

 
 Source notes and lineage
 The weekday–tithi yoga rules in this handbook are from the classical muhurta tradition as preserved in later manuals and modern compilations. This is Jyotiṣa/Muhūrta literature, not “Veda” in the strict Saṃhitā sense.
 
 Primary working source for the exact yoga tables: Ernst Wilhelm, Muhurta Yogas: Combinations of Vara, Tithi & Nakshatra. It lists the Siddha, Amrita, Krakacha, Dagdha, Hutasana, Visha, and Samvartaka Vāra–Tithi combinations.
 Tithi group framework: Ernst Wilhelm, Classical Muhurta, section on Nandā, Bhadrā, Jayā, Riktā, and Pūrṇā tithis.
 Classical tradition context: Muhūrta Cintāmaṇi and Kālaprakāśikā are part of the broader muhurta literature in which weekday, tithi, nakshatra, yoga, and karana combinations are examined for electional timing.
 
 Terminology note: The spellings “Tithi,” “Thithi,” “Vāra,” “Vaar,” “Amrit,” and “Amrita” are all commonly encountered in English-language Jyotiṣa material. This handbook uses Sanskrit-style transliteration in headings while keeping plain spellings searchable.
