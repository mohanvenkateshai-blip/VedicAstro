Introduction

It is sometimes disconcerting for beginning students of Jyotisha to learn
that there is more to consider in this system of astrology than just the birth
chart. Numerous divisional charts, or vargas are also utilized, all of which are
derived mathematically from the basic birth chart. Without them, as I once
heard my teacher put it, “Jyotisha becomes something of a crude affair.”
With them, it becomes sophisticated, precise, and breathtakingly accurate —
provided, of course, there is a very accurate birth time.

See this quote from Kalyan Verma, the author of the 13४५ century
Sanskrit classic, Saravali:

“The divisional charts hold the entire knowledge about the future of a
horoscope, without which it is impossible to lay a step into astrology”

This is a rather dramatic statement and suggests that without the use of
divisional charts predictive accuracy is impossible. An example will serve to
illustrate. Beginning students learn that the 7 house of the birth chart is said
to indicate marriage and the spouse. In keeping with this, marriage is said to
occur in the periods and sub-periods of planets associated with the 7" house
and the 7" lord. Yet practical experience shows that it is not always so. Sage
Parashara tells us that in addition to the 7® house of the birth chart, all matters
pertaining to marriage and the spouse also need to be seen through the
navamsha or the ninth divisional chart (D-9).

The Divisional Charts of Jyotisha

If you understand the basic astronomy of an astrological birth chart then
you realize that each constellation of the Zodiac takes about two hours to rise
up over the local horizon. This means that anyone born within that two-hour
period at the same locale will have very similar birth charts. Those skeptical
of astrology will invariably bring up the issue of twins, who are always born
within minutes of each other at the same place. Their horoscopes then are
usually even more identical, yet they often live very different lives. This
would appear to disprove the basic astrological premise that a person’s birth
chart reflects their unique destiny based on their past life karma.

Western astrology in my experience has no satisfactory answer to this
charge and no methodology for differentiating the fate of twins or others born
very nearly at the same time and place. Jyotisha does, and the answer lies in
the divisional charts. Even twins are born a few minutes of a part, and as we
saw in the example above, it gives rise to different sets of divisional charts

It should be very clearly understood that the sign placement of planets
in these divisional charts are all mathematical derivations of their position in
the birth chart. They are not based on astronomical factors per se, as in the
birth chart. This is true also of the lagnas for each of these charts, which are
likewise derived from the exact degrees and minutes of the birth ascendant.

Take for example, the navamsha, which is considered the most important

divisional chart. Navamsha means literally, “the 9" division” and so when a
sign, which is 30 degrees, is divided by nine, it yields nine distinct divisions
of 3 degrees, 20 minutes each, as follows:

Time Sensitivity of the Vargas

Students should clearly understand that the more subtle the division
chart, the more sensitive it 15 to even minor inaccuracies in the birth time.
This is particularly true for the lagna of each divisional chart, since it is based
on the most personal and rapidly changing aspect of a birth chart—the exact
degree of the ascendant. Since the navamsha is the most important divisional
chart, I will again use it to illustrate this point.

It takes approximately two hours for a whole sign of the Zodiac to
“ascend” over the horizon line. This means that every degree of a sign is
equal to about four minutes of clock time. Since each navamsha is 3 degree,
20 minutes, a different navamsha will appear on the horizon very 13 plus
minutes or 30.

If a person’s birth time gives a degree of the birth ascendant that is on
the “edge” of a particular navamsha, then even a minute or two of inaccuracy
will change the navamsha lagna and therefore give a whole different
patterning to this varga.

Return to the birth chart of Farah Fawcett and see that her degree of
ascendant at birth was 11-54 of Gemini. We saw that this falls in the 4"
navamsha, which runs from 10-00 to 13-20, and gives her a Capricorn
navamsha lagna. Had she been born a mere six minutes or so later, the
degree of her birth ascendant would have advanced past 13-20, putting it in
the 5% navamsha and giving her an Aquarius, not Capricorn lagna in this
divisional chart.

And say, for example, someone was using a birth time that gave 10-5 as
the degree of ascendant. If they were actually born just a minute earlier, the
navamsha lagna would shift back one, since this is right on the edge. (The 4"
navamsha runs from 10-00 to 13-20, while the 3 navamsha is from 6-40 to
10-00 of any sign)

Due to the relative imprecision with which birth times are recorded, even
in hospitals, this is a huge problem in Jyotisha. One can never just assume
that a time given from a person, even if it comes from a birth certificate, is
accurate enough to give a “true” set of divisional charts. Broader divisions

Sub-Groups of the 16 Vargas

Though Parashara gives these 16 divisions in the Brihat Parashara Hora
Shastra, in practice, they are not all widely used. This is partly due to problem
of inaccurate birth times referred to above, but also it appears that some of the
knowledge has been lost, or resides only in the oral tradition. In no book,
ancient or modern, have I seen an illustration of how the Hora should be used
to judge wealth, or even any reference to divisions such as D-40 or D-45. D-
60 or the shastyamsha, the finest division given here and equating to just two
minutes of clock time, is said to be particularly useful for differentiating the
separate destinies of twins. Yet even then the recorded times would need to be
very precise for this varga to be reliable that way.

Parashara also sub-divides these 16 divisions into four groups, and
assigns a relative value to each varga within these classifications, equaling a

The Most Basic Use of Divisional Charts—Seeing a Planet’s

One of the first things we learn as beginning students of Jyoftisha is the
different “happy” and “unhappy” conditions of planets, known as planetary
“dignities.” We learn that when a planet is “exalted” is tends to give stronger
and more favorable results, versus when it is “debilitated,” in which case it is
more likely to give weaker and less favorable results (with some notable
exceptions).

chart is not the whole story, and in one sense is more just the outward
appearance of the planet. The planet’s dignities in different vargas,

favorably a planet will be able to give its results in its periods and sub-

interpretation needs to be adjusted.

Assessing a Planet’s Strength in the Divisional Charts

And why is planetary strength such an important consideration? Simply
stated, without assessing this factor, one will never be able to judge correctly
to what degree the various planetary yogas in a chart will actually give their
promised results in their periods and sub-periods.

Parashara explicitly instructs us on this point, though it is easy to
overlook. After the chapters in the Brihat Parashara Hora Shastra on
planetary combinations that give position (raja yogas) and wealth (dhana
yogas), the Sage makes the following important qualifying statement:

“The yogas mentioned above should be delineated only after knowing the
favorable or unfavorable dispositions of the participant planets and their
strength and weakness.”

Chapter 41, verse 17
BPHS

Translation by R. Santhanam

---

## Page 29

Significantly, what follows in the next 16 verses is a discussion of the
auspicious results that occur when angle and trine lords obtain a number of
good dignities in the divisional charts.

From this sequencing, it would appear that the Sage is telling us to
evaluate the real results that a raja and dhana yoga can give based in part on
the participant planets falling in “good divisions.”

Note the flow of his exposition:

* First the Sage tells us the planetary combinations that will give fame
and fortune. Most of these involve the lords of angles and trines.

° Then he implies that the degree of fame and fortune will depend on
how strong these angle and trine lords are.

* He then suggests that this strength should be measured by how many
“good divisions” these angle and trine lords obtain in the divisional
charts.

In an earlier chapter Parashara defines “good divisions” as placement in:

1. Exaltation sign
2. Moolatrikona sign
3. Own sign

However, it is worth noting at this point that in another primary text,
Phaladeepika, the author, Mantreshwara, gives a more liberal interpretation
that includes “friend’s sign” as a “good division,” Other Sanskrit classics
support this view as well, such that for our purposes here, “Great Friend” and
“Friend” classifications should be added to our list of “good divisions.”

Predicting With Divisional Charts

Earlier in this lesson I quoted my teacher’s remark that “without the use
of the divisional charts, astrology becomes a rather crude affair.” Why? The

---

## Page 47

answer is partly what we have already seen. Two people born at nearly the
same time and place may have virtually identical birth charts, and without the
divisional charts there is no real way to differentiate their life experience.

However, the other reason is that all areas and aspects of a person’s life
happen concurrently. An individual may be running the major period of a
planet in his 7" house, emphasizing relationships, but, of course, at the same
time he or she is likely to be having a career, children, parental and sibling
relationships, hobbies, a home, friends, and so forth. At any one time, one
area of our life may be going very well, but another area may be falling apart.
We may have just received a job promotion, but also have a father in the
hospital dying of cancer.

This is the common experience of life, and to expect to be able to
astrologically “see” many different concurrent events based on just the
placement of the major period and sub-period in the birth chart would be
asking a lot. Fortunately, this is not the case with this highly sophisticated
form of astrology.

In Jyotisha, this is exactly why the divisional charts exist, and the
purpose for which they are most used. With them, an astrologer can assess
what a particular period and sub-period might bring in a specific area of a
person’s life. They are a very brilliant, intricate and breath-taking feature of
this science, and also one of its more frustrating, since rarely is a given birth
time accurate enough to give a complete set of accurate divisional charts.
However, as we shall see, they can also be used to rectify the birth time.

Some General Guidelines for Using the Divisional Chart

Among modern exponents of the use of divisional charts in Jyotisha, the
work of H.R. Seshadri Iyer stands out. These are given in his book New
Techniques of Prediction (Volume Two). For a full discussion of these,
students are referred to this work.

The following are what I consider to be some of the most important
principles given there, which I have summarized based on my own
understanding.

* Begin by realizing that a particular divisional chart is an additional and
more detailed view of a particular area of life indicated in a more
general way by one of the houses of the birth chart. For example,
whereas all factors influencing the 7" house/7™ lord of a birth chart
provide clues as to the person’s experience in spousal relationships, the
navamsha (D-9) supplements that view and provides more detail,
particularly with respect to timing of events.

---

## Page 48

In analyzing divisional charts, all the general elements of interpretation,
such as planetary dignities (exaltation, own sign, etc.), aspects,
hemming, directional strength, exchanges, yogas, and so forth should
be applied.

* Dashas will show timing—what a particular period and sub-period is
likely to bring in so far as the area of life to which the divisional chart
pertains i.e., saptamsha for experiences with children, dashamsha for
career life and so forth.

° Iyer emphasizes that the most important and necessary condition for a
planet to give beneficial effect in any divisional chart is its relative
placement from the lagna in that divisional chart. Here the well-known
classification of “good” and “bad” houses applies. Placement in an
angle house (1, 4, 7, 10,) or a trine house (1, 5, 9) is preferred, while
planets falling in 2 or 11 are more “neutral.” Placement in the
dussthana houses (3, 6, 8, or 12) is not considered as desirable.

Caution: Do not make a “dogma” of this statement but rather
use it as a general guideline.

° Iyer suggests some exceptions to the above, which should become the
focus for research and confirmation among students. Personally, I have
found them to be true.

1. Mars in the 6" house will give beneficial effect
2. Mercury in the 8" house of the chaturthamsha (D-4) will be good

* The magnitude of the effect is to be judged by the “strength” of the
planet. The most powerful placements are:

1. Exaltation

2. Vargottama

3. Own Sign

4. Directional Strength

° Neecha Bhanga or “cancellation of debility” and Neecha Bhanga Raja

* All the general yogas like Gaja Kesari and so forth should be seen in

Dashamsha (D-10) For Status and Achievements

Mathematically, this division is derived by dividing 30 degrees of a sign
by 10, which gives 10 divisions of 3 degrees each. Once a planet’s position
(or degree of the ascendant) is located within these 10 possible segments, the
counting starts from the planets own position in the birth chart if it falls in an
odd numbered sign, or 9" from its position if it falls in an even numbered
sign.

For example, a planet at 7 degrees of Aries falls in the 3™ dashamsha,
and since Aries is an even numbered sign, it will go into Gemini in the
dashamsha chart.

If a planet in the birth chart were at 7 degrees of Taurus, it would again
go into the 3" dashamsha, but since Taurus is an even sign, the counting

---

Capricorn puts it into Pisces in the dashamsha.

This division is like unto the 10™ house of the birth chart and pertains to
one’s status and achievements in the world —in other words—to one’s
profession and career life.

Summary of Guidelines for Using Divisional Charts

as a general principle (but don’t make an invariable law out of it!) that
when a planet falls in a good house in a divisional chart, it will give
more beneficial results in the matters pertaining to that division during
its dasha.

7. This will be particularly so if the planet is also strong by virtue of being
exalted, vargottama, in its own sign, or having directional strength. Be
careful to notice such conditions as planets exchanging signs, or planets
that may be deceptively strong by virtue of neecha bhanga raja yoga.

8. If such planets, falling under conditions 5 and 6 above, are additionally
involved in significant and benefic yogas, then they can bring
exceptionally good results, if this indication is also supported by the
birth chart and navamsha.

9. Planets falling in dussthana houses (3, 6, 8, 12) in a divisional chart
may not give their best results during their dashas in matters pertaining
to that division. However, in my experience they can give much better
results if they are in a good condition like exaltation, vargottama, own
sign etc.

10. The effects of a planet in a divisional chart get modified based on its
conjunction and aspect with other planets. Here the emphasis is on the
natural benefics and malefics, not the functional ones. The strong
influence of Jupiter on a planet in a divisional chart will significantly
enhance its ability to bring beneficial effects, even if it is placed in a”
bad” house.

11. When analyzing any divisional chart, see especially the lagna, lagna
lord, and then the house/house lord particularly pertaining to that
division. For example, the 3™ house of the drekkana for younger
siblings, the 7 house of the navamsha for spouse, the 5५ house of the
saptamsha for children, the 10" house/10® lord in the dashamsha for
career etc. Finally, see the relevant house from the karaka. For
example, the 3४ house in the drekkana from the karaka of younger
sibling, Mars; the 7" house in the navamsha from the karaka of
relationships, Venus, the 4" house in the dwadashamsha from the
karaka of mother, the Moon etc.

Jyotisha can feel overwhelming complex at times, and certainly the use
of divisional charts greatly adds to that complexity. Yet without them, the
wonderful subtly and true predictive power of the science gets lost. You
really would not be practicing Jyotisha as it was intended, and will not get the
most accurate results.

At the very least, take the most basic approach of seeing the house

position and condition of the dasha lord in a particular division, and judge the
resultant effect accordingly:

If it falls in a good house (angle, trine or 2, 11) and in a good condition,
it’s very good for the matters pertaining to that division.

If it falls in a good house but in an ordinary, or even bad condition, it’s
still good.

If it falls in a bad house (3, 6, 8, 12), but is in a good condition, it’s fair
to good. Connection with good benefics helps increase the beneficial
effect.

If it falls in a bad house, in an ordinary condition, it is not so good, but
connection with good benefics helps increase the beneficial effect.

If it falls in a bad house, in a bad condition, it is not good at all, but
connection with good benefics helps decrease the ill effects.

When planets are in a bad house, poor condition and additionally under
the influence of malefics, without the relief of benefics, it can give rise
to some of the more challenging effects. This is true for planets in the
birth chart and any divisional chart.

This concludes the lesson on the use of divisional charts for prediction,

but, as we shall see, they also serve another very useful purpose. Since they
are so sensitive to even minor inaccuracies in the birth time, they can be used
to verify a chart, or to correct the birth time and the degree of the ascendant, if
necessary.
